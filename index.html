My Story

Vedanti

Dance has always held a special place. I always used to stand in front of my television enjoying the rhythm of the music when my mother saw my passion for dance she introduced me to the world of Bharatnatyam when I was just six years old. My grandma who has always had a devotion for Bharatnatyam was something that passed on to me. I remember my first dance class vividly: the rhythmic beat of the mridangam, the melodious strains of the Carnatic music, and the intricate footwork that seemed almost magical. Little did I know that this was the beginning of a lifelong journey.

My initial years were spent mastering the basics. Under the teaching of my first guru, Mrs. Anu Shivram, I learned the importance of discipline, dedication, and the nuances of the dance form. The weekly 3-time rigorous practices, the repetitive adavus (basic steps), and the detailed lessons in mudras (hand gestures) and abhinaya (expressions) were fatiguing, yet they laid a solid foundation. These formative years were crucial in shaping my understanding and appreciation of Bharatnatyam.

As I grew older, my passion for Bharatnatyam deepened. I began participating in cultural programs and annual years. Each performance was a step forward, an opportunity to polish my skills and express myself. I was particularly drawn to the stories and mythology conveyed through Bharatnatyam. Characters like Lord Krishna, Lord Shiva, and Goddess Parvati came to life through dance, and I found immense joy in portraying these roles.

Once, I moved to the UK I had the honour of being taught by Mrs. Meena Rajagoplalan. Here, I was exposed to a wealth of knowledge and a rich legacy of dance. I had received a deeper understanding of the theoretical aspect of dance, learning about its history, its connection to temple rituals, and its evolution over the centuries.

Prayer

Pushpam means flowers and Anjali is to offer, Pushpanjali is an invocatory dance where the dancers offer flowers to the Almighty and seek blessings. They then offer their respects to their guru and the audience.

Pushpanjali, Shlokam and Alarippu

Pushpanjali will be followed by a Shlokam in praise of Lord Ganesha and Alarippu set to Misra Chappu Talam (7 beats). Pushpanjali in Ragam Gambheera Nattai and Adhi talam.

Jathiswaram

Jathiswaram is a nritta (pure dance) presentation without any abhinaya (emotions). This dance piece exhibits the execution of a Jathi (rhythmic pattern danced to narrated syllables), intricate footwork (adavus) and hand gestures (mudras) fused to beautiful sequences (korvais) according to the nature of the sequence of swara passages (musical notes) in a particular format.

Jathiswaram: Ragatalamalika

Oh principal among the gods (devas), the granter of benevolence. The Lord whose physical form rivals countless crores of cupids in beauty. You are the holy one who protects, guards, and nourishes the ascetics and countless thousands of crores of beings on this earth.

Oh lord, who is the essence of the Vedas. The very embodiment of the compassionate. Oh supreme lord, also known as Gangadhar, the one who bears the Ganga on his head by subduing her pride.

A very vibrant Keertham by Ambujam Krishna in praise of the dancing Lord Shiva of Chidambaram. You kept one foot on the earth and measured the sky with the second foot, and the third one you kept on the head of Mahabali. Devi

Om Hreem Shreem is a Sanskrit mantra, made up of one-syllable sounds known as bija, or seed mantras. Om is believed to be the sacred sound of the universe, Hreem is symbolic of divine goddess energy, and Shreem is the bija mantra of Lakshmi, goddess of abundance and prosperity.

This will be preceded by a few verses from The Rajarajeshwari Ashtakam hymn (stotra) composed by Adi Shankaracharya in praise of the goddess, who is considered a manifestation of the divine feminine known as Amba, Parvati, or Durga.

Hanuman Chalisa

Ragamalika Talam: Adi

A devotional hymn (stotra) in praise of Lord Hanuman, and popularly recited by millions of devotees every day. It is an Awadhi language text attributed to Tulsidas and is his best-known text apart from the Ramcharitmanas. The word Chalisa is derived from Chalisa meaning the number forty in Hindi, denoting the number of verses in the Hanuman Chalisa.

Javali

Ragam: Kamas
Talam: Adi

A Javali is a musical composition that is performed towards the end of a dance recital. It is believed that Javalis came into vogue around the early 19th century. A song in a lighter vein, the javali is a very popular form of classical music which is a favorite of both dancers and musicians.
